Week in Review

This week marked an inflection point for transcatheter valve therapy. The Wall Street Journal published a front-page investigative feature by Betsy McKay describing the rapid growth of TAVR explant surgeries — the fastest-growing cardiac operation in the United States by rate — and the patients who got TAVR younger than guidelines recommend and are now back on the operating table years later. In the same news cycle, the presidents of the Society of Thoracic Surgeons, American College of Cardiology, and AATS met personally with CMS Administrator Mehmet Oz to advocate for the Heart Team model and evidence-based valve care. And on Wednesday, Edwards Lifesciences reported a blockbuster Q1 2026 — 12.7% sales growth, raised guidance, $500M buyback — driven precisely by the TAVR expansion the WSJ piece scrutinized.

The convergence of mainstream press scrutiny, professional society advocacy at the federal payer level, and record industry growth is itself the story of the week. Layered on top: the Tricuspid Valve Academic Research Consortium published TVARC-2 in JACC, establishing standardized trial design for the next generation of tricuspid studies; a meta-analysis of 13,308 patients found TEER inferior to surgery for degenerative mitral regurgitation; and the cardiology world lost Eugene Braunwald at age 96 — the founder of modern cardiology, whose hemodynamic work on aortic stenosis and mitral regurgitation laid the foundation for everything that followed.

If you read one section this week, read the top story.

Top Stories This Week

1. [NOTABLE] Wall Street Journal Front-Page Feature: TAVR's Risky Tradeoffs Meet Coordinated Society Advocacy at CMS

On Thursday evening, the Wall Street Journal published Betsy McKay's investigative feature, "A Breakthrough Heart Procedure Comes With Risky Tradeoffs," followed Friday by a companion patient-facing primer, "What Heart-Valve Patients Need to Know Before Treatment." Together, they constitute the most prominent mainstream press scrutiny of TAVR in the procedure's 15-year history.

The reporting documents what structural heart surgeons have been warning about for years: TAVR has expanded well beyond the elderly, high-risk population it was approved for in 2011. According to the article, nearly one-third of patients ages 40 to 65 who need aortic valve replacement now receive TAVR — a population in which both ACC/AHA 2020 and ESC 2025 guidelines recommend SAVR unless surgical risk is prohibitive. The piece profiles three patients: Susan Glannan, age 60 at her TAVR, back on the operating table at 64 for an explant; Leon Schiefer, 55 at his TAVR, in heart failure four years later requiring explant plus three-vessel CABG; and Jane Booth, 65, healthy and asymptomatic, told by her surgeon she'd "get depressed" recovering from surgery.

The data: TAVR explants are the fastest-growing cardiac surgery in the US by rate, with explant volumes climbing sharply since 2020 according to STS data cited in the piece. Dr. Shinichi Fukuhara of University of Michigan, who has performed more than 175 TAVR explants, told McKay: "Almost nobody's thinking about what's next." The piece quotes Dr. Vinay Badhwar (STS President), Dr. Lars Svensson (Cleveland Clinic), and Dr. Martin Leon (Columbia, Co-PI of PARTNER 3) on durability concerns in younger patients.

What makes this a single story rather than two: the same week the WSJ published, the presidents of STS, ACC, and AATS met personally with CMS Administrator Mehmet Oz to advocate for the Heart Team model and evidence-based valve care. Vinay Badhwar's LinkedIn post on April 23 tagged Oz, Roxana Mehran, Emile Bacha, Raymond Singer, and Michael Mack, writing: "The Heart Team is where science, judgment, and humanity converge to deliver the best outcomes." In a follow-up comment, Badhwar shared the WSJ link with: "Let us remain united on the value of evidence to guide practice." This was not coincidental coverage. It was a coordinated push that has reached the desk of the Administrator who controls valve reimbursement.

Editorial perspective: The WSJ feature has limitations — three patient anecdotes are not data, and Dr. Raj Makkar's counterpoint that "age isn't a cutoff" is a defensible position within the ACC/AHA shared-decision-making framework for ages 65–80. But the piece accurately reflects the trajectory: Wells Fargo projects TAVR revenue growing from $7.02B in 2025 to $9.91B by 2030, an expansion implicitly built on continued penetration of younger and lower-risk patients. The new ESC 2025 guidelines lowered the TAVI-preferred threshold to age 70 for patients with tricuspid AV anatomy and suitable transfemoral access — but they explicitly note that patients under 70 are poorly represented in randomized trials, and BAV patients (disproportionately younger) were excluded from virtually all RCTs comparing TAVI with SAVR. The convergence of WSJ scrutiny, society advocacy at CMS, and the same week's Edwards Q1 results showing TAVR driving record growth is precisely the tension this newsletter exists to surface. Readers should expect this story to develop — CMS engagement at this level is rarely ceremonial.

2. [NOTABLE] Edwards Lifesciences Q1 2026 Earnings: Record TAVR Growth Meets Mainstream Skepticism

Edwards reported Q1 2026 revenue of $1.65B, up 12.7% year-over-year, beating consensus estimates with adjusted EPS of $0.78 versus $0.73 expected. Edwards raised full-year 2026 guidance and announced a $500M accelerated share buyback. Both TAVR and TMTT (transcatheter mitral and tricuspid therapies) drove growth.

Analyst response was overwhelmingly positive: Barclays raised its target to $110, Truist raised its target on TAVR growth, Goldman Sachs raised to $98, Evercore ISI to $93, and BTIG raised on the Q1 beat. Canaccord was the lone contrarian, cutting on valuation.

Editorial perspective: The earnings story cannot be read separately from the WSJ story. The same growth narrative driving Edwards' beat — expansion into younger, lower-risk, and asymptomatic patients — is the trajectory the WSJ piece scrutinizes. The forward P/E of 25x prices in continued double-digit TAVR volume growth, which mathematically requires either further expansion of indications (beyond the 2024 asymptomatic AS approval, into moderate AS and bicuspid valves), expansion to younger patients, or both. If the STS/ACC/AATS-CMS conversation translates into payer scrutiny, or if the next ACC/AHA guideline update tightens age thresholds rather than loosening them, the growth math gets harder. Edwards shares actually fell 2.2% on Thursday despite the beat — a possible early signal that the market is already discounting the regulatory and reputational risk. By Friday, EW closed at $84.15, up 5.56% on the day, recovering as the buyback and raised guidance digested.

3. [NOTABLE] TVARC-2 Published in JACC: Standardizing Tricuspid Trial Design

The Tricuspid Valve Academic Research Consortium published TVARC-2 in the Journal of the American College of Cardiology on Wednesday — a consensus document on trial design and endpoint selection for transcatheter tricuspid valve studies. Authors include Davidson, Hahn, Mehaffey, Cohen, and Pinney. The inclusion of J. Hunter Mehaffey — a surgeon who has published critically on therapies outpacing evidence — alongside high-profile interventional cardiologists signals an attempt at balanced trial architecture.

The document arrives at a critical moment. The 2025 ESC/EACTS guidelines elevated transcatheter tricuspid valve treatment to Class IIa (LOE A) for high-risk symptomatic severe TR, based on TRILUMINATE Pivotal, Tri.Fr, and TRISCEND II. But these trials used different primary endpoints, different TR grading systems, and different comparator arms. TVARC-2 standardizes the design framework for technology-vs-technology, technology-vs-surgery, and technology-vs-medical-therapy comparisons.

Editorial perspective: Standardization is overdue. Real-world TVT Registry data show 30-day mortality of 3.1% and new-pacemaker rates of 15.9% with transcatheter tricuspid replacement — meaningful complication signals in a procedure designed for symptomatic relief, not mortality reduction. A single-center Vancouver cohort published this week (Jelisejevas et al., CJC Open) showed 12-month mortality of 29% (T-TEER) and 34% (TTVR), far exceeding trial reports. TVARC-2 establishes the scaffolding to evaluate the next wave of tricuspid trials more rigorously than the first wave was evaluated.

4. [NOTABLE] TEER vs. Surgery for Degenerative MR: Meta-Analysis of 13,308 Patients Strongly Favors Surgery

Kurmasha et al. published a systematic review and meta-analysis with trial sequential analysis comparing TEER with surgical mitral repair or replacement in degenerative (primary) mitral regurgitation. The findings are unambiguous:

  • 1-year mortality: 82% higher with TEER (RR 1.82, 95% CI 1.04–3.19)
  • ≥2-year survival: Significantly favored surgery (RR 0.72, 95% CI 0.56–0.93)
  • Reintervention at ≥1 year: 4.5× higher with TEER (RR 4.52)
  • MR reduction to grade 0: Dramatically inferior with TEER (RR 0.20)
  • TEER advantages: Lower new-onset AF, less transfusion, shorter hospital and ICU stays

Editorial perspective: Both ACC/AHA 2020 and ESC 2025 are clear that surgical repair is Class I for primary MR in operable patients; TEER is Class IIa, restricted to high surgical risk. This meta-analysis reinforces that hierarchy emphatically. Caveats apply — mixing of RCTs and observational cohorts, residual confounding, EVEREST II using first-generation MitraClip — and the landmark REPAIR-MR and PRIMATY trials will provide the definitive modern answer. Until then, the data should restrain any narrative that TEER is a substitute for surgery in operable primary MR.

5. In Memoriam: Eugene Braunwald (1929–2026)

Eugene Braunwald, the founding father of modern cardiology, died at 96. His contributions to valvular heart disease — the hemodynamic foundations of mitral regurgitation, aortic stenosis pathophysiology, the design of the TIMI trials, the textbook Braunwald's Heart Disease that has trained five generations of cardiologists — built the scaffolding on which today's transcatheter era stands. The field has lost an irreplaceable giant.

Aortic Valve (TAVR/TAVI)

Beyond the WSJ-Edwards-CMS convergence, multiple aortic valve studies this week deepened both the optimistic and skeptical narratives.

Evolut Low Risk 5-Year Isolated Cohort

Ramlawi et al. in Annals of Thoracic Surgery published a post hoc analysis of the Evolut Low Risk Trial restricted to isolated procedures (667 TAVR, 497 SAVR). At 5 years, death or disabling stroke was virtually identical (15.5% vs 14.6%, P=0.84). However, the permanent pacemaker rate was 27.3% with TAVR vs 8.9% with SAVR. The composite headline is reassuring; the pacemaker disparity is a persistent Achilles' heel, particularly relevant for younger patients with decades of expected life.

Atrial Fibrillation and TAVI Outcomes (CENTER2, n=23,320)

Hemelrijk et al. presented the largest analysis to date of AF in the TAVI population, pooled from 10 international studies. Pre-existing AF (28.2% prevalence) carried adjusted HR 1.39 for mortality. New-onset AF (6.2% incidence) was even more impactful — HR 1.75 for mortality, doubled major bleeding, near-doubling of 1-year stroke. This is clinically actionable: post-TAVI rhythm monitoring may need to be more systematic, and the NCT07519161 trial from Shanghai East comparing metoprolol vs. amiodarone for NOAF prevention is directly relevant.

BAV-TAVI in Younger, Low-Risk Patients (AD-HOC Registry)

Gorla et al. reported on 980 BAV patients undergoing TAVI in the international AD-HOC registry. In the youngest, lowest-risk subgroup (Group I: <69 years, STS-PROM <4, n=113), technical success was 94.7% with no severe valve deterioration during follow-up. However, both ACC/AHA (Class IIb) and ESC 2025 (Class IIb) rate BAV-TAVI cautiously, NOTION 2 showed numerically higher events with TAVI in BAV patients (HR 3.8, p=0.07), and patients "deemed unsuitable for surgery" represent a highly selected subset. Magyari et al. published a parallel single-center series of 52 BAV patients treated with the Myval THV with comparable outcomes to trileaflet patients but a 34% pacemaker rate.

Cerebral Embolic Protection: Still No Proven Stroke Benefit

Gbreel et al. meta-analyzed 8 RCTs (11,589 patients): no significant reduction in overall, disabling, or non-disabling stroke with CEPDs during TAVI. A modest, transient improvement in MoCA scores at 2–5 days disappeared at later follow-up. Trial sequential analysis indicates current evidence is insufficient to definitively confirm or refute benefit. Against widespread CEPD adoption, this remains a significant evidence gap.

Pediatric AVR Durability: A Stark Reminder

Yokota et al. in Circulation: Cardiovascular Interventions reported on 180 pediatric AVR procedures: freedom from reintervention at 10 years was 29% for bioprosthetic valves vs. 82% for mechanical (HR 4.66, P<0.001). Pediatric metabolism accelerates structural valve deterioration, but the directional message is one the WSJ piece also raised: bioprosthetic valves do not last forever, and the durability question for TAVR in patients aged 50–70 over 15–20 years remains unanswered.

TAVR-MET: Tirzepatide and Valve Healing

Thirugnanam et al. reported the TAVR-MET trial: 260 obese TAVR patients randomized to perioperative tirzepatide vs. placebo. At 6 months, tirzepatide reduced subclinical leaflet thrombosis (8.4% vs 21.6%) and ≥mild paravalvular leak (10.7% vs 25.3%). Provocative concept, but open-label design, surrogate endpoint (HALT on 4D-CT), and short follow-up demand caution. A retrospective TriNetX database study (3,416 propensity-matched patients) likewise found GLP-1 RA use associated with lower MACE (HR 0.63) and mortality (HR 0.61) post-TAVR — effect sizes plausibly inflated by healthy-user bias and residual confounding. MedPage Today covered both stories. The hypothesis is interesting; the data require RCT validation before practice change.

Other Aortic Findings This Week

  • SAVER amyloidosis screening: Prospective cohort of 1,001 AS patients — multi-organ screening flagged 40% of patients; 2% had confirmed cardiac amyloidosis. Predictors: male sex (OR 23.8), carpal tunnel (OR 5.5), spinal stenosis (OR 4.1), sparkling myocardium (OR 4.8). AUC 0.88. Pre-TAVI amyloid screening should become routine.
  • Post-TAVI readmissions and mortality (n=1,008): Naito et al. — at 10 years, all readmission types carry HRs of 4.5–5.7 for mortality. Fracture readmissions show a year-2 secondary peak.
  • Women have better long-term TAVI survival: TAVI-NOR registry, n=600 — propensity-matched women had HR 0.66 for events.
  • VR-augmented TAVI planning RCT: Circulation: Cardiovascular Imaging — VR planning reduced access-site bleeding vs. standard CT planning (n=140).
  • Leadless pacemakers post-TAVI (MITAVI): 102 French patients — 38% recovered AV conduction (suggesting we may implant too early), only 2 major complications, no TR risk from leads.
  • Redo-TAVR coronary obstruction risk: Liccardo et al. — in small annuli (≤430 mm²), self-expanding valves carry OR 15.5 for predicted CO at redo-TAVR vs. balloon-expandable. Index-procedure choice matters for lifetime management.
  • Unmeasured confounders in young TAVR studies: Huang et al. correspondence in Annals of Thoracic Surgery echoes the surgical community's concern about indication creep below evidence-supported thresholds.
  • India context: Deccan Herald reported TAVR costs ~4× SAVR in India — a global health equity dimension under-discussed in Western literature.

Mitral Valve (Repair & Replacement)

TEER for Degenerative MR: Surgery Wins

The Kurmasha et al. meta-analysis (covered as Top Story #4) is the headline mitral story of the week. TEER carried 82% higher 1-year mortality and 4.5× higher reintervention compared to surgical repair/replacement in degenerative MR. Both ACC/AHA and ESC remain clear that surgery is Class I for primary MR in operable patients; this meta-analysis reinforces that hierarchy.

Acute Hypoxemic Respiratory Failure After M-TEER in Patients With Concomitant AS

Sonbol et al. in Catheterization and Cardiovascular Interventions reported a single-center series of 238 M-TEER patients: concomitant mild-to-moderate AS was independently associated with acute hypoxemic respiratory failure within 24 hours (16.7% vs 3.8%, adjusted OR 4.38). Mechanism: acute afterload increase as MR reduction unmasks fixed AS physiology. Hypothesis-generating, single-center, but practically relevant for patient selection and post-procedural monitoring.

TEER Complications

Amsalem et al. documented mitral leaflet tears after TEER — a recognized but underemphasized complication. JACC Case Reports described retrieval of an embolized DragonFly clip from a 10-cm giant left atrium using a passive-capture snare strategy after active "chasing" failed. As TEER volumes expand under the ESC 2025 Class I upgrade for ventricular SMR, complication transparency must keep pace with enthusiasm.

Atrial Functional MR: TEER vs. Medical Therapy

From the OCEAN-Mitral and REVEAL-AFMR registries (reference literature), TEER for moderate or severe atrial functional MR was associated with lower all-cause mortality and HF hospitalization vs. medical therapy (HR 0.65, P=0.044). The ESC 2025 guidelines for the first time formally define atrial SMR as a distinct entity — this remains an emerging space.

TMVR: Large Annulus and the Late-Referral Problem

Siki and Kaneko published an editorial in Annals of Thoracic Surgery: "Large Annulus Equals Poor Outcomes in TMVR: Are We Seeing These Patients Too Late?" The Medtronic Intrepid TMVR Pivotal trial continues recruiting toward 1,056 patients. Funaki et al. reported feasibility of TEER in adults with surgically repaired congenital heart disease from the OCEAN-mitral registry — a niche but growing population.

Tricuspid Valve (Repair & Replacement)

TVARC-2: The Trial Design Framework

Covered as Top Story #3. The TVARC-2 publication in JACC establishes standardized endpoints and trial designs — the scaffolding the rapidly expanding tricuspid space has lacked.

T-TEER vs. TTVR: First Direct Comparative Real-World Data

Jelisejevas et al. in CJC Open reported the first direct comparison of patient profiles and outcomes between T-TEER (n=14) and TTVR (n=29) from a single Vancouver center:

  • TTVR patients were younger (76 vs 80) but anatomically more complex (higher GLIDE scores)
  • 30-day mortality: 0% T-TEER vs. all early deaths in TTVR group
  • 12-month mortality: 29% T-TEER vs. 34% TTVR
  • HF readmission at 12 months: 37% overall

The 12-month mortality of 29–34% in both groups is sobering — substantially exceeding TRILUMINATE Pivotal (9.3%) and TRISCEND II (~12%). The sample is tiny and selection-driven, but these real-world numbers should inform shared decision-making.

ELASTA-T: Rescue Technique for Failed T-TEER

Alvarez-Covarrubias et al. in EuroIntervention (German Heart Centre Munich) described ELASTA-T — Electrosurgical Laceration and Stabilisation of T-TEER — for converting failed T-TEER to TTVR when centrally placed clips obstruct valve replacement deployment. Borrowing from BASILICA and LAMPOON principles, the technique uses a modified flying-V wire to electrosurgically lacerate leaflet tissue. Technically impressive — but the need for rescue strategies this early in a Class IIa-recommended therapy underscores how nascent the tricuspid lifetime-management framework is.

TTVR Real-World Registry Coverage

Medical Dialogues and Diagnostic and Interventional Cardiology covered the previously published STS/ACC TVT Registry analysis: 1,034 procedures, 98.4% implantation success, 3.1% 30-day mortality, 15.9% new-pacemaker rate in pacemaker-naïve patients. Mirrors the trial data, but 30-day mortality of 3% in a procedure designed for symptomatic relief — and a 16% pacemaker rate — should remain meaningful counterweights to enthusiasm.

Surgical Tricuspid Repair: Anatomy-Based Approaches

Arai and Grau in EJCTS published a comprehensive review unifying surgical tricuspid repair strategies — edge-to-edge, leaflet augmentation, papillary muscle relocation, annular repositioning — into a practical anatomical framework that "can rival evolving transcatheter options." A reminder that surgical innovation continues in parallel.

New Tricuspid Replacement Trials

The TRICENTO G2 first-in-human trial was registered (Medira GmbH, n=10), adding to a crowded TTVR pipeline alongside Edwards EVOQUE, TRiCares, and others. Meanwhile, the NHLBI/Cook TRAIPTA early feasibility study for trans-atrial intra-pericardial tricuspid annuloplasty has been suspended without specified reason.

Surgical vs. Transcatheter Comparisons

This week's evidence broadly reinforced surgical advantages where they have always existed, while underscoring transcatheter advances where evidence supports them:

  • Primary MR: Kurmasha et al. meta-analysis shows surgery clearly superior to TEER for survival, MR reduction, and reintervention.
  • Pediatric AVR: Yokota et al. shows mechanical valves have 4.7× lower reintervention than bioprosthetic — relevant analogically to younger TAVR patients.
  • Low-risk AS at 5 years: Evolut Low Risk isolated cohort shows equivalent death/disabling stroke (15.5% vs 14.6%) but 27% pacemaker rate with TAVR vs 9% with SAVR.
  • Acute severe primary MR: Lohse et al. reported on TEER vs surgery in acute severe primary MR from a cardiac ICU experience — full data awaited.
  • Sutureless vs. stented surgical AVR: SAVI-AVR trial protocol outlines a prospective non-randomized comparison addressing optimization within the surgical pathway.
  • Surgical tricuspid repair vs transcatheter: Arai and Grau argue the surgical armamentarium can rival transcatheter when fully deployed — head-to-head data awaited.

The ACC/AHA-vs-ESC age threshold disagreement (65 vs. 70 for SAVR-preferred floor; 80 vs. 70 for TAVI-preferred ceiling) remains the central battleground, and no data this week resolved it. The WSJ feature explicitly highlighted this gap in the patient-facing primer, citing surgeons including Cleveland Clinic's Lars Svensson advocating for SAVR in patients under 65 who would benefit from longer durability.
