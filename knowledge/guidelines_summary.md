# Valve Guidelines Knowledge Base: ACC/AHA 2020 vs ESC/EACTS 2025

**Purpose:** Editorial reference for The Valve Wire. This document summarizes the key recommendations from both guidelines, highlights where they agree and disagree, and identifies the critical decision points between surgical, transcatheter, and medical therapy.

**Sources:**
- **ACC/AHA 2020:** Otto CM et al. 2020 ACC/AHA Guideline for the Management of Patients With Valvular Heart Disease. J Am Coll Cardiol. 2021;77:e25-197.
- **ESC/EACTS 2025:** Praz F, Borger MA et al. 2025 ESC/EACTS Guidelines for the Management of Valvular Heart Disease. Eur Heart J. 2025;46:4635-4736.

---

## 1. Aortic Stenosis: The Central Battleground

### 1.1 When to Intervene

Both guidelines agree on the core indications:

| Indication | ACC/AHA 2020 | ESC 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| Symptomatic severe high-gradient AS | Class I | Class I |
| Symptomatic low-flow low-gradient AS, reduced LVEF | Class I | Class I |
| Asymptomatic severe AS, LVEF <50% | Class I | Class I |
| Asymptomatic severe AS during other cardiac surgery | Class I | Class I |

**Key Expansion in ESC 2025:** Early intervention (IIa, LOE A) is now recommended as an alternative to watchful waiting in ALL asymptomatic patients with severe high-gradient AS and low procedural risk. This is based on four RCTs: EARLY TAVR, RECOVERY, AVATAR, and EVoLVeD. The ACC/AHA 2020 guidelines had early intervention only for specific subgroups (very severe AS, elevated BNP, abnormal exercise test).

### 1.2 SAVR vs TAVI: The Age Threshold Disagreement

This is the single most consequential disagreement between the two guidelines:

**ACC/AHA 2020 Framework:**
- **<65 years:** SAVR recommended (Class I)
- **65-80 years:** Either SAVR or TAVI, shared decision-making (Class I)
- **>80 years or life expectancy <10y:** TAVI preferred (Class I)

**ESC 2025 Framework:**
- **<70 years:** SAVR recommended if surgical risk is low (STS-PROM + EuroSCORE II <4%) (Class I)
- **>=70 years with tricuspid AV:** TAVI recommended if suitable anatomy for transfemoral approach (Class I)
- **All remaining BHV candidates:** SAVR or TAVI per Heart Team (Class I)

**The 65-70 Gap:** The ESC effectively expanded the TAVI-preferred zone by 10 years vs ACC/AHA, while also raising the SAVR-preferred floor by 5 years. Patients aged 65-70 sit in a "shared decision" zone in the ACC/AHA but in a "SAVR preferred" zone in the ESC. Conversely, patients aged 70-80 are in a "shared decision" zone in ACC/AHA but TAVI-preferred in the ESC.

**Why the ESC shifted:** The DEDICATE trial, longer follow-up from PARTNER 3 and Evolut Low Risk (up to 5-10 years), and meta-analyses showing no systematic durability difference between TAVI and SAVR at available follow-up periods. However, the ESC explicitly notes that patients <70 are poorly represented in RCTs.

### 1.3 Factors Favoring SAVR vs TAVI

| Favors SAVR | Favors TAVI |
|---|---|
| Younger age / longer life expectancy | Older age / shorter life expectancy |
| Bicuspid aortic valve | Tricuspid AV with suitable anatomy |
| Subaortic (LVOT) calcification | Porcelain aorta |
| Rheumatic disease | Prior chest radiation |
| Small or large annulus (need enlargement) | Prior CABG with at-risk grafts |
| Concurrent aortic dilation requiring surgery | No transfemoral access contraindication |
| Severe CAD requiring CABG | Non-complex CAD (PCI feasible) |
| Concern for patient-prosthesis mismatch | Mobility issues (high sternotomy risk) |
| Mechanical valve preferred | Bioprosthetic valve preferred |
| Need for concomitant myectomy | Frailty likely to improve post-TAVI |

### 1.4 Bicuspid Aortic Valve

Both guidelines agree BAV remains primarily surgical territory:
- **ACC/AHA 2020:** TAVI for BAV is Class IIb
- **ESC 2025:** TAVI for BAV at increased surgical risk with suitable anatomy is Class IIb

BAV patients were excluded from virtually all RCTs comparing TAVI with SAVR. NOTION 2 included 100 BAV patients and showed a numerically higher event rate with TAVI (7 vs 2 events, HR 3.8, P=0.07). SAVR is preferred especially if patients are young, have coexistent aortopathy, or unfavourable valve morphology (heavy calcification, calcified raphe).

### 1.5 Durability Considerations

ACC/AHA 2020 emphasized that robust TAVI durability data extended only to ~5 years. The ESC 2025 notes that RCTs and observational data now show comparable SVD rates between TAVI and SAVR at up to 10 years, though acknowledges potential selection/survival bias and variable SVD definitions.

**Lifetime management** is a major ESC 2025 addition: careful CT-based anatomical analysis at the index procedure is paramount when life expectancy exceeds assumed valve durability, considering:
- Future valve-in-valve feasibility (coronary obstruction risk, sinus sequestration, PPM)
- THV explantation remains high risk (12-17% mortality)
- TAV-in-SAV increases PPM risk
- Neo-skirt height and commissural alignment matter for future TAV-in-TAV

### 1.6 Key Trials Referenced

| Trial | Population | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|
| PARTNER (2010) | Inoperable/high risk | TAVI superior to medical therapy |
| PARTNER 2 (2016) | Intermediate risk | TAVI non-inferior to SAVR |
| PARTNER 3 (2019) | Low risk | TAVI non-inferior; early benefit at 1y |
| Evolut Low Risk (2019) | Low risk | TAVI non-inferior to SAVR |
| DEDICATE (2024) | Low risk, investigator-initiated | TAVI non-inferior; death+stroke 5.4% vs 10.0% at 1y |
| EARLY TAVR (2024) | Asymptomatic severe AS | Early TAVI reduced composite vs surveillance |
| RECOVERY (2020) | Asymptomatic very severe AS | Early SAVR reduced mortality |
| AVATAR (2022) | Asymptomatic severe AS | Early SAVR reduced composite |

## 2. Mitral Regurgitation

### 2.1 Primary MR

Both guidelines strongly favor surgical MV repair over replacement. Key points of agreement:
- Symptomatic severe PMR: surgery recommended (Class I)
- Asymptomatic with LV dysfunction (LVEF <=60%, LVESD >=40mm): surgery recommended (Class I)
- Repair preferred over replacement when durable result expected (Class I)

**Key Disagreement -- Early Repair in Asymptomatic Patients:**
- **ACC/AHA 2020:** Class IIa, requires >95% repair success rate and <1% expected mortality
- **ESC 2025:** Upgraded to **Class I** if at least 3 of: AF, SPAP >50mmHg, LA dilation (LAVI >=60 mL/m2 or diameter >=55mm), concomitant TR >=moderate

This is a meaningful expansion -- more asymptomatic patients will be referred for surgery under ESC guidelines.

**TEER for Primary MR:** Both guidelines rate TEER as Class IIa for high-risk symptomatic patients with suitable anatomy. TEER remains subordinate to surgery for primary MR.

### 2.2 Secondary MR (Ventricular)

**The COAPT Upgrade:** This is the second most impactful disagreement between the guidelines.

- **ACC/AHA 2020:** TEER is **Class IIa** (reasonable) for ventricular SMR with COAPT-like criteria
- **ESC 2025:** TEER is **Class I, LOE A** (recommended) to reduce HF hospitalizations and improve QoL

The upgrade is based on:
- COAPT 5-year follow-up confirming sustained benefit
- RESHAPE-HF2 showing 36% reduction in HF hospitalization/CV death
- Meta-analysis of COAPT, MITRA-FR, and RESHAPE-HF2

**COAPT Criteria (ESC Table 7):**
- Anatomy suitable for M-TEER
- NYHA >=II
- LVEF 20-50%
- LVESD <=70mm
- SPAP <=70mmHg
- At least one HF hospitalization in prior year or elevated natriuretic peptides
- No severe RV dysfunction, advanced HF, CAD requiring revascularization

**MITRA-FR vs COAPT Discrepancy:** Different patient populations explain the divergent results. MITRA-FR enrolled patients with greater LV dilation and less severe MR (disproportionate MR concept). COAPT selected patients with proportionate (severe) MR relative to LV size.

### 2.3 Atrial Secondary MR (New ESC 2025 Entity)

The ESC 2025 formally defines atrial SMR as a distinct entity:
- Preserved LVEF (>=50%) without wall motion abnormalities
- No or mild LV dilation
- Mitral annulus dilation (AP >35mm)
- LA enlargement (LAVI >34 mL/m2)

Treatment: MV surgery + surgical AF ablation + LAAO (Class IIa); TEER if inoperable (Class IIb). The ACC/AHA 2020 briefly mentioned this phenotype but without formal definition or management pathway.

## 3. Tricuspid Regurgitation: The Emerging Frontier

### 3.1 The Biggest Change: Transcatheter Tricuspid Therapy

**ACC/AHA 2020:** Did not address transcatheter TR therapy (trials not yet completed).

**ESC 2025:** Transcatheter TV treatment is **Class IIa, LOE A** for high-risk symptomatic severe TR despite optimal medical therapy, without severe RV dysfunction or pre-capillary PH. This is based on:
- TRILUMINATE Pivotal: TR TEER reduced composite endpoint vs medical therapy, driven by QoL improvement and reduced HF hospitalizations at 2 years
- Tri.Fr: TR TEER + GDMT superior to GDMT alone for composite driven by PROMs
- TRISCEND II: Transcatheter TV replacement superior for symptoms/QoL (but higher bleeding/pacemaker risk)

### 3.2 Surgical TR Recommendations

Both guidelines agree on concomitant TV surgery during left-sided operations for severe TR (Class I). Key upgrades in ESC 2025:

| Recommendation | ACC/AHA 2020 | ESC 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| TV surgery for symptomatic severe primary TR | IIa | **Class I** |
| TV surgery for asymptomatic severe primary TR with RV dilation | IIb | **IIa** |
| TV surgery for symptomatic secondary TR with RV dilation | IIa (annular dilation type only) | **IIa** (broader) |

### 3.3 The Too-Late-Referral Problem

Both guidelines emphasize that patients are often referred too late for TR surgery, when RV failure and end-organ damage have occurred. Historical isolated TV surgery mortality was 8-20%, but contemporary cohorts with earlier referral show improved outcomes. The ESC 2025 provides a detailed stepwise evaluation framework and expanded grading (massive, torrential) beyond the traditional severe grade.

## 4. Aortic Regurgitation

This remains largely a surgical domain. Both guidelines agree:
- Symptomatic severe AR: surgery recommended (Class I)
- Asymptomatic severe AR with LV dysfunction: surgery recommended (Class I)

ESC 2025 additions:
- Valve-sparing aortic root replacement recommended in young patients (Class I)
- AV repair should be considered at experienced centres (Class IIa)
- **TAVI for AR:** Class IIb for inoperable patients with suitable anatomy (new)

---

## 5. Mitral Stenosis

Recommendations are largely unchanged. PMC remains first-line for rheumatic MS with favorable anatomy. ESC 2025 adds TMVI (Class IIb) for degenerative MS with MAC at experienced centres, acknowledging the high-risk nature of all interventions in this population.

## 6. Decision Trees Summary

### Aortic Stenosis Decision Tree
```
Severe AS confirmed
  |
  +-- Symptomatic? --> YES --> Intervention indicated (Class I)
  |     |
  |     +-- Age <70 (ESC) / <65 (ACC/AHA) --> SAVR
  |     +-- Age >=70 (ESC) / >80 (ACC/AHA) with tricuspid AV + TF access --> TAVI
  |     +-- 65-80 (ACC/AHA) / remaining (ESC) --> Heart Team: SAVR or TAVI
  |     +-- High/prohibitive surgical risk --> TAVI if survival >12mo
  |     +-- Survival <12mo --> Palliative care
  |
  +-- Asymptomatic?
        |
        +-- LVEF <50% --> Intervention (Class I)
        +-- Very severe AS / rapid progression / elevated BNP / LVEF <55% / abnormal exercise
        |     --> Early intervention (IIa in ESC; varies IIa-IIb in ACC/AHA)
        +-- None of above --> Watchful waiting with 6-month follow-up
```

### Mitral Regurgitation Decision Tree
```
Severe Primary MR
  |
  +-- Symptomatic --> MV surgery (repair preferred) (Class I)
  +-- Asymptomatic with LV dysfunction (LVEF <=60%, LVESD >=40mm) --> MV surgery (Class I)
  +-- Asymptomatic, preserved LV, 3+ risk factors (AF, SPAP, LA, TR) --> MV repair (ESC: Class I)
  +-- High surgical risk --> TEER (IIa)

Severe Ventricular Secondary MR
  |
  +-- Optimize GDMT + CRT first
  +-- Persistent symptoms on GDMT + meets COAPT criteria --> TEER (ESC: Class I / ACC: IIa)
  +-- With CAD requiring CABG --> MV surgery (ESC: Class I / ACC: IIa)
  +-- Not meeting COAPT criteria --> Consider TEER for symptoms (IIb) or LVAD/HTx
```

### Tricuspid Regurgitation Decision Tree
```
Severe TR
  |
  +-- Undergoing left-sided surgery --> Concomitant TV surgery (Class I)
  +-- No left-sided surgery indication
        |
        +-- Primary TR, symptomatic, no severe RV dysfunction --> TV surgery (ESC: Class I)
        +-- Secondary TR, symptomatic, no severe RV/LV dysfunction or PH
        |     +-- Operable --> TV surgery (IIa)
        |     +-- High surgical risk --> Transcatheter treatment (ESC: IIa)
        +-- Severe RV dysfunction / pre-capillary PH --> Medical therapy
```

## 7. Critical Thresholds Quick Reference

| Parameter | Threshold | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| STS-PROM + EuroSCORE II | <4% | ESC definition of "low surgical risk" for SAVR preference |
| Aortic velocity (Vmax) | >=4.0 m/s | Severe AS threshold |
| Aortic velocity (Vmax) | >=5.0 m/s | Very severe AS; triggers early intervention consideration |
| Mean AV gradient | >=40 mmHg | Severe AS (high gradient) |
| AVA | <=1.0 cm2 | Severe AS |
| LVEF | <50% | Triggers intervention in asymptomatic severe AS (Class I) |
| LVEF | <55% | Triggers consideration for early intervention in AS (ESC IIa) |
| LVEF | <=60% | Triggers MV surgery in asymptomatic severe MR |
| LVESD | >=40 mm | Triggers MV surgery in asymptomatic severe MR |
| BNP | >3x normal | Triggers early intervention consideration in AS |
| Vmax progression | >=0.3 m/s/year | Rapid progression; triggers intervention consideration |
| Tricuspid annulus | >=40 mm or >21mm/m2 | Triggers concomitant TV repair during left-sided surgery |
| COAPT: LVEF | 20-50% | Eligibility for TEER in ventricular SMR |
| COAPT: LVESD | <=70 mm | Upper limit for TEER in ventricular SMR |
| COAPT: SPAP | <=70 mmHg | Upper limit for TEER in ventricular SMR |
| CT calcium score (male) | >2000 AU | Confirms severe AS in low-flow low-gradient |
| CT calcium score (female) | >1200 AU | Confirms severe AS in low-flow low-gradient |

## 8. Editorial Angles for The Valve Wire

### The Big Stories
1. **TAVI at 70:** ESC lowered the age threshold for TAVI as primary treatment to 70, potentially expanding TAVI volumes significantly in Europe and influencing US practice before the next ACC/AHA update.

2. **MitraClip Goes Class I:** TEER for ventricular SMR is now the highest-level recommendation in Europe. The COAPT-MITRA-FR discrepancy is resolved by patient selection (proportionate vs disproportionate MR).

3. **Tricuspid Revolution:** From zero transcatheter TR recommendations in 2020 to Class IIa in 2025. TRILUMINATE, Tri.Fr, and TRISCEND II created an entirely new treatment category.

4. **End of Watchful Waiting?** The EARLY TAVR trial has pushed early intervention for asymptomatic AS into mainstream guideline recommendations (IIa). Combined with RECOVERY and AVATAR, the paradigm is shifting toward earlier treatment.

5. **Atrial SMR Recognized:** For the first time, atrial secondary MR has a formal definition and management pathway, acknowledging that AF-driven MR is a different disease from ventricular SMR.

### Ongoing Tensions
- Durability uncertainty beyond 10 years for TAVI limits enthusiasm for younger patients
- BAV remains a TAVI frontier with limited data
- The appropriate role of transcatheter TV replacement vs repair
- Whether TEER benefit in SMR extends beyond COAPT-like patients
- Lifetime management: how to plan for future reinterventions at the index procedure

### Watch For
- Next ACC/AHA update expected to incorporate DEDICATE, EARLY TAVR, TRILUMINATE data
- NCT04204915 and NCT03972644 (additional asymptomatic AS trials)
- NCT05051033 (TEER vs surgery for PMR in patients >60)
- Long-term TAVI durability data beyond 10 years
- Real-world BAV TAVI outcomes
