Today's Key Findings

[NOTABLE] The multicenter Japanese registry of 4,623 TAVR patients, including 700 nonagenarians, demonstrates that chronological age alone should not determine TAVR candidacy. Five-year mortality was higher in patients ≥90 years (53.2% vs 37.0%), but this excess was driven primarily by noncardiovascular deaths. Importantly, cardiovascular mortality was similar between age groups (20.3% vs 17.0%, P=0.198). Frailty and malnutrition emerged as the strongest prognostic factors, with preserved nutrition and low frailty identifying nonagenarian subgroups achieving favorable long-term survival.

This finding challenges current practice patterns that use age cutoffs for TAVR candidacy, aligning with both 2020 ACC/AHA and 2025 ESC guidelines' emphasis on comprehensive geriatric assessment rather than chronological age alone.

Aortic Valve (TAVR/TAVI)

The Japanese nonagenarian TAVR registry published in JACC Advances provides compelling evidence for individualized risk assessment. With 700 patients aged ≥90 years followed for 5 years, the study found that multivariable analysis identified frailty (Clinical Frailty Scale) and malnutrition (Geriatric Nutritional Risk Index) as independent predictors of mortality, while age ≥90 years was not. The Clinical Frailty Scale-Geriatric Nutritional Risk Index heatmap revealed marked heterogeneity among nonagenarians, supporting the authors' conclusion that "TAVR candidacy in nonagenarians should be guided on geriatric assessment of frailty and nutrition to identify patients most likely to achieve meaningful survival while avoiding futile interventions."

ACC Scientific Sessions featured the PRO-TAVI study, examining whether high-risk patients should undergo PCI before TAVR. Results suggest comparable outcomes when deferring PCI before TAVR in elderly patients, potentially simplifying the treatment pathway.

The PROTECT H2H study presented head-to-head comparisons of embolic protection devices during TAVR, comparing the Emboliner versus Sentinel systems—addressing the ongoing debate about cerebral protection efficacy.

A new Medtronic study highlights AI alerts for optimizing TAVR treatment timing in heart valve disease, representing the growing integration of artificial intelligence in procedural planning.

Tricuspid Valve (TriClip, TTVR)

Abbott's new study demonstrates that TriClip treatment reduces heart failure hospitalization risk, adding to the evidence base for transcatheter tricuspid repair. This builds on data from the TRILUMINATE Pivotal trial, which showed significant tricuspid regurgitation reduction with the TriClip device.
