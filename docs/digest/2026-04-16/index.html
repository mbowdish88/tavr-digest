Executive Summary

Today's most impactful finding comes from JAMA Cardiology: a post hoc analysis of the EVoLVeD trial shows that myocardial fibrosis burden predicts adverse outcomes in asymptomatic severe aortic stenosis patients, but — critically — the benefit of early intervention did not significantly differ based on fibrosis severity, complicating the narrative that fibrosis-guided referral can precisely identify who benefits most from early TAVR. Meanwhile, a major JACC: Cardiovascular Interventions cluster features the GASS-TAVR geriatric assessment score for predicting futility after TAVR, a NOTION-3 substudy showing PCI harms frail TAVR patients without benefit, and a landmark TVT Registry analysis of 5,401 mitral valve-in-valve cases revealing that low post-procedural gradients paradoxically predict worse survival. The Evolut Low Risk 5-year isolated-procedure data are published in Annals of Thoracic Surgery, and a troubling Annals study documents that nearly half of the lowest-surgical-risk patients under 65 are receiving TAVR — directly counter to both ACC/AHA and ESC guidelines.

For clinicians parsing today's data: the EVoLVeD fibrosis analysis reinforces the trend toward early intervention in asymptomatic AS (now ESC Class IIa) but reminds us that no single biomarker cleanly stratifies treatment benefit. The NOTION-3 frailty substudy adds nuance to the concomitant PCI-plus-TAVR strategy — a win in non-frail patients, a liability in frail ones. The mitral valve-in-valve gradient data upend conventional wisdom and demand a rethinking of post-MVIV echocardiographic benchmarks. And the under-65 TAVR utilization data should concern every Heart Team: practice is outpacing evidence in the very population where long-term durability matters most.

Today's Key Findings

[NOTABLE] EVoLVeD Fibrosis Analysis (JAMA Cardiology): In 224 asymptomatic severe AS patients with midwall fibrosis on CMR (median follow-up 42 months), higher fibrosis burden was associated with increased composite events (HR 1.23 per 1% increase, 95% CI 1.08–1.37), driven by unplanned AS-related hospitalizations. However, no significant interaction existed between fibrosis burden and randomization arm (P for interaction = 0.39). In the high-fibrosis subgroup, early intervention numerically reduced the primary endpoint (20% vs 32%) and significantly reduced hospitalizations (7% vs 25%, HR 0.27). In the low-fibrosis subgroup, no difference was seen. Editorial note: This is a post hoc subgroup analysis of a modest-sized trial — hypothesis-generating, not definitive. The absence of a statistically significant interaction means we cannot conclude that fibrosis selectively identifies responders to early intervention, despite the tantalizing point estimates. The ESC 2025 guidelines already reference EVoLVeD among the trials supporting early intervention (IIa) in asymptomatic severe AS, but fibrosis-guided patient selection remains unvalidated prospectively.

[NOTABLE] Hospital-Level Variation in TAVR vs SAVR in Patients Under 65 (Annals of Thoracic Surgery): Among 13,907 aortic valve replacements in adults under 65 (Vizient database, 2018–2023), a striking 55.8% received TAVR. Even among the lowest-risk patients (predicted mortality <0.5%), 46.5% underwent TAVR. Hospital-level variation was enormous (median odds ratio 2.69), suggesting non-clinical factors — institutional culture, referral patterns, volume incentives — are driving procedure choice in the very population where both ACC/AHA (<65 = SAVR, Class I) and ESC (<70, low risk = SAVR, Class I) recommend surgery. This mirrors concerns raised by Badhwar and others about indication creep outpacing evidence, particularly given limited TAVR durability data beyond 10 years and the exclusion of younger patients from major RCTs.

[NOTABLE] Transmitral Gradients After Valve-in-Valve (JACC: Cardiovascular Interventions): In 5,401 patients from the STS/ACC TVT Registry undergoing transcatheter MVIV (2015–2024), low discharge transmitral gradient (<4 mmHg) was paradoxically associated with higher 3-year mortality compared to intermediate (4–7 mmHg; adjusted HR 1.52, P = 0.0002) and high gradients (>7 mmHg; adjusted HR 1.35, P = 0.01). Low-gradient patients had lower cardiac output and cardiac index, suggesting the gradient reflects hemodynamic status rather than valve function. The authors caution against using discharge gradient alone to judge procedural success — a direct challenge to current clinical reflexes.

[NOTABLE] NOTION-3 Substudy: PCI in Frail TAVR Patients (JACC: Cardiovascular Interventions): Among 407 NOTION-3 patients with available frailty data (32% frail), PCI reduced MACE, all-cause mortality, MI, and urgent revascularization in non-frail patients (HR 0.42, P < 0.001). In frail patients, PCI provided no benefit and increased bleeding (HR 2.51, P = 0.011). Caveat: Frailty was assessed post hoc using a calculated score, not a pre-specified validated instrument. The subgroup sizes are small (130 frail patients). These findings require prospective confirmation but suggest a "less is more" approach to revascularization in the frailest TAVR candidates.

Aortic Valve (TAVR/TAVI)

Evolut Low Risk 5-Year Isolated Procedure Outcomes

Ramlawi et al., Annals of Thoracic Surgery report 5-year outcomes comparing isolated TAVR versus isolated SAVR from the Evolut Low Risk trial. The abstract provided is limited, but this analysis is important because the original trial included patients with concomitant procedures — isolating TAVR-vs-SAVR removes confounders introduced by combined PCI or other interventions. These data join PARTNER 3 and DEDICATE in building the 5-year evidence base for low-risk TAVR, though the ESC 2025 guidelines already shifted the TAVI-preferred threshold to age ≥70 based in part on earlier Evolut follow-up. Notably, SAVR remains preferred below age 70 (ESC) or 65 (ACC/AHA) given unresolved durability questions beyond a decade.

GASS-TAVR: A Geriatric Assessment Score for Futility

Fumagalli et al. (JACC: Cardiovascular Interventions) developed and validated a geriatric assessment-based score predicting 1-year mortality or functional decline in 562 consecutive TAVR candidates ≥75 years across three Italian centers. The composite endpoint occurred in 13.9% of patients. Predictors included nutritional status (Mini Nutritional Assessment), baseline functional dependence (BADL), renal function, and pulmonary artery pressure. The score achieved an AUC of 0.92 in derivation and 0.87 in validation — substantially outperforming STS-PROM. This is a welcome addition to the futility prediction literature, though it requires external validation in non-Italian cohorts and prospective testing before clinical adoption. The accompanying editorial (Mattace-Raso) contextualizes the growing recognition that procedural success is necessary but not sufficient — functional benefit must be the benchmark.

Cerebral Embolic Protection in High-Calcium TAVR

Pawar et al. (JACC: Cardiovascular Interventions) examine the effectiveness of cerebral embolic protection devices during TAVR in patients with high aortic valve calcium burden. Full data are not available in the abstract, but this addresses an important question: whether CEP devices provide differential benefit in the highest-risk anatomy for embolization. Current data on CEP remain mixed (the PROTECTED-TAVR trial showed a signal for clinical stroke reduction but did not reach significance for the primary endpoint), and risk-stratified analyses like this may help identify patients who derive the most benefit.

Conduction Disturbances: Persistent Is What Matters

Okita et al. (International Journal of Cardiology) analyzed 1,007 TAVI patients across two centers and found that persistent conduction disturbances (present at discharge or beyond 7 days) — but not transient or pre-existing ones — independently predicted the composite of cardiovascular death or heart failure hospitalization at 2 years (log-rank P = 0.023). Persistent CD occurred in 11.1% of patients. This has practical implications for post-TAVI monitoring: transient conduction abnormalities that resolve before discharge may not warrant the same level of concern or pacemaker implantation, though prospective validation is needed before changing threshold-to-implant protocols.

Cardiac Tamponade After TAVI: A 12-Year Experience

Hussein et al. (International Journal of Cardiology) report on 29 tamponade events among 2,354 TAVI procedures at a single UK center (incidence 1.2%), with 51.7% mortality at 90 days. The incidence declined from 2.3% (2013–2018) to 0.9% (2019–2025, P = 0.004), driven mainly by elimination of RV perforation through LV wire pacing adoption. Annular rupture remained the dominant mechanism (51.7%) and trended toward highest mortality (66.7% at 90 days). All but one case involved balloon-expandable valves. These data underscore that while tamponade is rare and declining, annular rupture remains a devastating event — reinforcing the need for meticulous CT-based annular sizing, particularly as TAVR moves into lower-risk populations where complication tolerance is lower.

TAVR Reintervention: Failure Mechanism Does Not Predict Outcomes

Penta et al. (Circulation: Cardiovascular Interventions) report from the EXPLANTORREDO-TAVR registry (553 patients, 29 centers) that structural valve deterioration (SVD, 65%) and non-structural valve dysfunction (NSVD, 35%) had distinct profiles — SVD occurred later (50.7 vs 5.5 months), was more common in balloon-expandable valves, and favored non-BEV at redo-TAVR — but neither failure mechanism predicted differential mortality after redo-TAVR or TAVR-explant. Importantly, 30-day mortality for TAVR-explant remained high at 12–16% regardless of mechanism, consistent with prior data establishing explant as a high-risk procedure. Redo-TAVR mortality was substantially lower (1.7–3.2% at 30 days). These data inform lifetime management planning — a central concern of the ESC 2025 guidelines — and support the emerging preference for redo-TAVR when anatomically feasible.

Delayed Coronary Obstruction From Pannus Formation

Perrard et al. (European Heart Journal: Case Reports) describe a rare case of delayed coronary obstruction caused by early pannus overgrowth around a self-expandable THV (Evolut Pro+) just 6 months post-TAVI, causing recurrent NSTEMI. Neither chimney stenting nor explant was deemed feasible; the patient underwent CABG. This case highlights an underappreciated failure mode that may become more relevant as cumulative TAVR implant numbers grow and patients survive longer with their prostheses.

TAVR vs SAVR for Aortic Regurgitation: Early Single-Center Data

Zhang et al. (Cardiology Research and Practice) retrospectively compared 37 TAVR and 33 SAVR patients with aortic regurgitation at low-to-intermediate risk (single Chinese center, 2021–2024). TAVR showed shorter procedure times, less blood use, shorter stays, and zero 30-day mortality versus four SAVR deaths (P = 0.029). Major caveats: This is a tiny, non-randomized, single-center study with no mention of propensity matching or adjustment for selection bias. AR remains "Class IIb for inoperable patients" in ESC 2025, and the JenaValve ARTIST trial (NCT06608823) is the first RCT designed to compare TAVR versus SAVR for AR. Until ARTIST data are available, these results should be viewed as hypothesis-generating at best.

TAVI Expansion in India

ET HealthWorld reports on the expanding role of TAVI in India, framing it as a minimally invasive innovation redefining heart valve care. While TAVI adoption in emerging markets is an important access story, the same guideline principles apply: age-appropriate patient selection, Heart Team decision-making, and realistic expectations about durability in younger populations are essential regardless of geography.

Mitral Valve (MitraClip, PASCAL, TMVR)

The Gradient Paradox After Mitral Valve-in-Valve

The Abbas et al. TVT Registry analysis (detailed above) is the day's most clinically disruptive mitral finding. A low transmitral gradient (<4 mmHg) after MVIV reflects low cardiac output — not excellent valve function. The accompanying editorial by Pilgrim and Tomii contextualizes this "gradient paradox," arguing that cardiac output must be integrated into post-MVIV assessment. This parallels the well-known low-flow, low-gradient paradox in aortic stenosis and should change how echocardiographers report post-MVIV hemodynamics. The recent Heart Valve Collaboratory framework on imaging transcatheter valve failure is directly relevant here.

5-in-1 Transcatheter Mitral Valve Replacement

Camaj et al. (JACC: Cardiovascular Interventions) describe a combined transcatheter procedure involving mitral valve replacement with ventricular and atrial septal closure using electrosurgery. Abstract details are limited, but this reflects the growing complexity of catheter-based structural interventions and the trend toward addressing multiple pathologies in a single session — an approach that trades procedural efficiency against cumulative risk in what are typically high-risk patients.

APSC Consensus on TEER for Mitral and Tricuspid Regurgitation

Yeo et al. (JACC: Asia) present updated Asian Pacific Society of Cardiology consensus statements on transcatheter edge-to-edge repair for both MR and TR. Sixteen consensus statements were developed using GRADE methodology. This is notable as a regional adaptation effort — the Asia-Pacific region has distinct epidemiology (more rheumatic disease, younger patients, variable access to surgery) that may appropriately shift the risk-benefit calculus for TEER, though the same evidence limitations apply.

Tricuspid Valve (TriClip, TTVR)

Real-World Evoque TTVR Data: Reassuring but Early

TCTMD reports that real-world Evoque transcatheter tricuspid valve replacement data are "reassuring" — consistent with the recently published STS/ACC TVT Registry analysis showing 98.4% implantation success, 3.1% 30-day mortality, and significant functional improvement. The headline captures the right nuance: it's still early days. The ESC 2025 guidelines gave transcatheter TR treatment a Class IIa recommendation based on TRILUMINATE, Tri.Fr, and TRISCEND II, but long-term durability and the optimal threshold for intervening in TR (repair vs replacement) remain unresolved. New CIED implantation (15.9%) and bleeding (7.9%) rates in the TVT Registry were actually lower than the TRISCEND II pivotal trial, which is encouraging but may reflect real-world patient selection differences.

Acute Hemodynamic Instability After TTVR

JACC: Cardiovascular Interventions publishes on the frequency and outcomes of acute hemodynamic instability following TTVR — a complication unique to transcatheter tricuspid replacement that has garnered attention as volumes increase. The right ventricle is preload-sensitive, and abruptly eliminating severe TR can unmask RV dysfunction. This remains an active area of investigation and underscores the importance of pre-procedural RV assessment — the ESC 2025 guidelines explicitly exclude patients with severe RV dysfunction from transcatheter TR treatment recommendations.

Surgical vs. Transcatheter Comparisons

Today features several important comparisons. The under-65 TAVR utilization study (Glance et al., Annals of Thoracic Surgery) is the most concerning: a 2.7-fold variation in TAVR-vs-SAVR odds across hospitals suggests institutional culture — not patient anatomy or risk — is driving decisions in younger patients. When 46.5% of the lowest-risk patients under 65 receive TAVR despite both major guidelines recommending surgery, we are witnessing the kind of indication creep that Kaul, [critic], and others have warned about. The lifetime management implications are real: these patients will likely need reintervention, and the EXPLANTORREDO-TAVR data published today remind us that explant carries 12–16% mortality while redo-TAVR, though safer, creates progressive patient-prosthesis mismatch concerns.

The Evolut Low Risk 5-year isolated-procedure data (Ramlawi et al.) will need careful scrutiny for the specific outcomes in patients under 65 and under 70 — the populations where the SAVR-vs-TAVR debate is most consequential. The ESC 2025 shifted the TAVI-preferred threshold to ≥70 partly based on earlier Evolut data, but whether 5-year follow-up is sufficient to inform lifetime decisions remains the central unresolved question.

The single-center TAVR-vs-SAVR comparison for aortic regurgitation (Zhang et al.) shows favorable short-term TAVR metrics but is far too small and methodologically limited to shift practice. AR remains surgical territory, with TAVR only IIb for inoperable patients (ESC 2025). The ARTIST trial (JenaValve) is the study to watch here.

Device & Technology

Vela Martín et al. (Journal of Invasive Cardiology) report a case of catastrophic right coronary artery dissection after surgical removal of coronary chimney stents following valve-in-valve TAVI. While a single case report, it illustrates the cascading complexity of managing failed transcatheter valves — each reintervention layer adds risk. The ESC 2025 guidelines emphasize that lifetime management planning at the index procedure is paramount, including assessment of future coronary access. This case is a cautionary example of what happens when that planning falls short.

A new trial (NCT07524595) will compare the self-expanding Hydra THV series versus balloon-expandable devices (SAPIEN, MyVal) for valve-in-valve TAVI in failed surgical bioprostheses — addressing the growing question of optimal valve-in-valve strategy, including the supra-annular design advantages of self-expanding platforms for avoiding patient-prosthesis mismatch.
